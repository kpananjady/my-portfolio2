In the aftermath of the 2012 Delhi gangrape, female MPs were some of the most vocal advocates for change. This time around, MPs have been accused of politicizing the issue and alienating those across the aisle — particularly when Smriti Irani said that opposition leader Rahul Gandhi condoned rape by mentioning the word in a speech condemning the prime minister's silence on the matter. Here's a representation of what polarization looks like among women in parliament — represented by the yellow dots — and what opportunities for regional coalition building may exist, represented by the purple dots. Circle are sized based on the number of edges going in and out of each node; also it's worth pointing out that while the absolute numbers of women MPs might differ between the two major parties, the BJP and the Congress, as a percentage of ther members the parties are not so far apart at 14 and 11 percent respectively.

it's pretty clear that the BJP (the largest yellow circle) not only has the largest share of women in the Lok Sabha, they also have geographic monopolies (purple dots that they own). When you consider that about half of the parties on this list are part of their ruling alliance, you can see why the BJP feels comfortable alienating the opposition.
